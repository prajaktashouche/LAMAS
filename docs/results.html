Results

We ran a simulated play of 500 runs to obtain a quantitative analysis. The strategy we went with is to use random choice of either passing the turn, or placing a random card, if the player does not have the expected card in their hand. Otherwise, the player will always truthfully place the card if they have it in their hand. The runs were done on two different types of deals, as given below.

Discussion

We were able to see that in certain situations it is beneficial to the player to lie to win the game but it depends on the deal. For example: Alice has a higher chance of winning for deal-1 while 100% of losing in deal-2. It does not matter if she employs the strategy of lying or honesty in the latter case.

We have also seen that passing a turn can update the possible worlds to a situation where such a model cannot exist in reality. This can happen in manipulative updates as the player believes a dishonest statement to be true and thus there is a contradiction of the possible worlds with the real world.

We were also able to notice that bluffs are not called by the other players when the player has announced a statement truthfully, as no bluff is called unless the model is completely valid (and it is always the case that after a truthful announcement the model is valid).

Future Work

  • Increase the number of cards dealt to the players. This will increase the complexity and hence also the winning rate from a strategy of lying.
  • Increase the number of cards that can be placed at a turn. Even if the number of cards in hand are increased, we can always get the same results if the players are only placing one card at each turn. So increasing the number of cards to place will also provide more opportunities to get away with bluffing.
  • Increase the number of players.
  • Allow players to call bluffs manipulatively. As players only call bluffs when they are completely sure of them, and they all know this fact, calling a bluff actually informs other players about the caller's own state.
